Why I Plan to Pursue Graduate Studies in Financial Engineering

My interest in financial engineering crystallized during a Computational Finance course in my sophomore year, when the curriculum shifted from John C. Hull’s intuition-driven exposition to Steven Shreve’s Stochastic Calculus for Finance. Working through Itô’s Lemma revealed how assumptions about volatility translate directly into hedging errors and pricing accuracy. For the first time, I saw markets as mathematically structured systems where rigor is indispensable because real capital is at stake.

That realization exposed gaps in my preparation. During my internship at Trade Vectors, I processed equity data and backtested trading signals, but many strategies deteriorated out-of-sample or proved highly sensitive to parameter choices. While I could implement models, I lacked the theoretical depth to diagnose why they failed. To address this, I completed QuantNet’s C++ Programming for Financial Engineering course with Distinction, where building pricing engines and numerical routines from first principles emphasized computational efficiency and numerical stability.

My most significant academic growth emerged through research on recession forecasting under Dr. Manoj Kumar Yadav. While analyzing U.S. Treasury yield spreads, I identified systematic gaps in bond maturities during recessionary periods that conventional imputation methods treated as random noise, distorting cross-tenor correlations. By reformulating the problem using Wasserstein Gradient Flows, I preserved distributional geometry more effectively, leading to materially different economic insights. This experience taught me that progress in quantitative research comes not from optimization tricks but from choosing mathematics that respects the nature of the data.

These experiences have clarified my goal of pursuing quantitative research at the intersection of stochastic modeling and risk analysis. Graduate studies in financial engineering represent the essential next step to deepen my theoretical foundations while maintaining the connection to practical implementation that has defined my development.

What Motivates Me to Apply to the Baruch MFE Program

Baruch MFE’s blend of practical focus and academic rigor makes it ideal for my development. Three aspects particularly motivate my application.

First, the curriculum directly addresses challenges I have encountered. The core sequence in stochastic calculus, numerical methods, and computational finance provides the mathematical foundations I need to move beyond empirical pattern recognition toward principled model development. Courses like Risk Management and Algorithmic Trading Strategies connect to my industry experience at Trade Vectors and QuantEdge, where I learned that successful quantitative trading requires understanding execution frictions, transaction costs, and changing market regimes.

Second, Baruch’s faculty work on problems aligned with my research interests. Professor Dan Stefanica’s expertise in numerical methods for derivative pricing addresses computational challenges I encountered when building pricing engines. His focus on Monte Carlo simulation directly relates to the balance between accuracy and efficiency that defines production quantitative systems. Professor Peter Carr’s research on volatility modeling and exotic options connects to my work with stochastic differential equations, where I have seen how the same mathematical frameworks govern both image despeckling and financial derivatives.

Third, Baruch’s location in New York City and strong industry connections provide unparalleled access to the quantitative finance ecosystem. The practicum requirement ensures exposure to real problems from leading firms, offering the practical validation essential for developing research that creates measurable impact.

The combination of rigorous coursework, faculty expertise, industry integration, and the collaborative culture among students creates an environment where mathematical precision and market reality continually inform one another. This balance is what I have sought throughout my undergraduate years, and Baruch MFE represents the natural continuation of that journey.

My Career Goals Upon Graduation

My goal is to work as a quantitative researcher at a leading proprietary trading firm, developing systematic strategies that identify market inefficiencies through rigorous statistical methods and machine learning. I am drawn to proprietary trading because ideas are evaluated through real performance rather than theoretical appeal.

In my first two years after graduation, I plan to build production-quality strategies that account for transaction costs, execution frictions, capacity constraints, and changing market regimes. My experience has shown me that models often fail not because the mathematics is incorrect, but because practical constraints are misunderstood or ignored. I am particularly interested in combining machine learning with traditional quantitative methods in ways that remain interpretable and robust across different market conditions. The technical depth from Baruch MFE will prepare me to work on problems requiring mastery of stochastic modeling, numerical optimization, and market microstructure simultaneously.

By years three to five, I aim to take on greater responsibility in strategy development and contribute to the intellectual culture of my team. I hope to mentor junior researchers, helping them navigate the transition from academic training to production systems just as senior colleagues will guide me early in my career. I also intend to maintain connections with the academic community through Baruch’s alumni network and ongoing research collaborations.

Ultimately, I am driven by the belief that financial markets, though seemingly chaotic, possess underlying structure that can be understood and modeled using the right mathematical frameworks. I aim to build a career at this intersection of theory and practice, applying mathematical rigor alongside practical insight to improve market efficiency and understanding. The Baruch MFE program offers the technical depth, industry exposure, and collaborative environment necessary to pursue this vision with clarity and purpose.
