Do Past Winners Keep Winning? Understanding Momentum in Stocks

Posted on January 17, 2026

One of the most surprising and well-documented discoveries in financial markets is that stocks which performed well in the recent past often continue to perform well in the near future, while poor performers tend to keep underperforming. This phenomenon, known as momentum, contradicts the intuitive belief that prices instantly adjust to new information and instead suggests that trends can persist over intermediate time horizons.

Momentum is typically observed over periods ranging from three to twelve months and has been shown to generate abnormal returns across different market conditions. In a landmark 1993 paper, Narasimhan Jegadeesh and Sheridan Titman studied U.S. equities and demonstrated that a simple strategy of buying recent winners and selling recent losers produced statistically significant excess returns that could not be easily explained by traditional risk measures.

This finding posed a direct challenge to the Efficient Market Hypothesis, which asserts that asset prices fully reflect all available information and that past price movements should have no predictive power over future returns. The persistence of momentum profits suggested that markets may not be perfectly efficient and that systematic patterns in returns can exist for extended periods of time.

Behavioral explanations offer one of the most compelling interpretations of momentum. Investors may underreact to new information such as earnings announcements or macroeconomic news, causing prices to adjust gradually rather than instantaneously. Additionally, herding behavior and performance chasing can amplify existing trends, allowing momentum to persist before eventually reversing.

Today, momentum is one of the most widely used factors in quantitative and factor-based investing and has been observed across global equity markets, commodities, currencies, and other asset classes. However, momentum strategies are not without risk, as they are particularly vulnerable to sharp reversals during periods of market stress or sudden changes in investor sentiment.

Original Paper: Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993)

Why Low-Risk Stocks Can Outperform: Understanding the Volatility Anomaly

Posted on January 23, 2026

According to traditional finance theory, investors should be compensated with higher expected returns for bearing higher risk. In classical asset pricing models, volatility is often treated as a proxy for risk, implying that stocks with greater price fluctuations should outperform safer, more stable assets over the long run. However, extensive empirical evidence from real-world markets frequently contradicts this intuition.

In an influential paper, Ang, Hodrick, Xing, and Zhang examined the relationship between stock volatility and future returns and uncovered a striking result: stocks with higher idiosyncratic and total volatility tended to earn significantly lower average returns than their low-volatility counterparts. This negative relationship persisted even after controlling for size, value, and market exposure.

This counterintuitive pattern is known as the low-volatility anomaly and has been documented across multiple markets, including U.S. and international equities, as well as over long historical periods. Rather than being a short-lived market inefficiency, the anomaly appears to be a stable and pervasive feature of asset returns.

Behavioral explanations provide one compelling interpretation of this phenomenon. Investors may systematically overpay for volatile, lottery-like stocks that offer a small chance of extremely high payoffs. This preference can drive prices of high-volatility stocks above their fundamental values, resulting in lower subsequent returns, while relatively boring, stable stocks remain underpriced.

These insights have had a significant impact on modern portfolio construction and investment products. They have contributed to the rise of low-volatility and minimum-variance ETFs, as well as defensive equity strategies that seek to improve risk-adjusted returns by emphasizing stability rather than aggressive risk-taking.

Original Paper: The Cross-Section of Volatility and Expected Returns (Ang et al., 2006)

Why Value Works: Cash Flows vs Discount Rates

Posted on January 31, 2026

Stock prices are notoriously hard to predict. But here's something less obvious: even when prices move, we often can't tell why they moved. Did earnings expectations change? Or did investors just start demanding higher returns?

Campbell and Shiller's 1988 paper "The Dividend–Price Ratio and Expectations of Future Dividends and Discount Factors" gives us a way to separate these two forces. The insight turns out to be crucial for understanding why value investing works.

The Present-Value Identity

Start with something uncontroversial: a stock's price equals the present value of its future dividends.

\[ P_t = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{E_t[D_{t+j}]}{(1+r_{t+j})^j} \]

where \(P_t\) is today's price, \(D_{t+j}\) represents future dividends, and \(r_{t+j}\) is the discount rate applied by investors. This identity must hold regardless of how prices move over time.

Why the Dividend–Price Ratio Matters

Campbell and Shiller focus on the dividend–price ratio, \( D_t / P_t \), because it tells us whether stocks look cheap or expensive relative to current cash flows. But here's the thing: a high dividend–price ratio could mean two very different things.

  • Investors expect future dividends to grow slowly
  • Investors demand a higher expected return

The price alone doesn't tell you which. That's the problem Campbell and Shiller solved.

Separating Cash-Flow News from Discount-Rate News

Using a log-linear approximation, the authors show that unexpected returns can be decomposed into two components:

\[ r_{t+1} = \text{Cash-Flow News}_{t+1} - \text{Discount-Rate News}_{t+1} \]

Cash-flow news reflects revisions in expectations about future dividends, while discount-rate news captures changes in expected returns. Here's the key distinction: discount-rate changes are mostly temporary (they affect valuations but not fundamentals), while cash-flow news has lasting effects.

What the Data Reveals

Empirically, Campbell and Shiller find that movements in the dividend–price ratio predict future stock returns but do not strongly predict future dividend growth. This implies that most valuation changes are driven by time variation in expected returns.

Translation: when stocks look cheap (high D/P), it's usually because discount rates are high—not because dividends are about to crash. And when stocks look expensive (low D/P), it's because discount rates are low.

Implications for Value Investing

This framework completely reframes value investing. Value stocks outperform not because their businesses suddenly improve, but because they're priced to deliver higher returns. The "value premium" is baked into prices as compensation for risk.

It reconciles two things that seemed contradictory: return predictability (value works) and market efficiency (prices reflect available information). Both can be true if risk premiums vary over time.

Original Paper: The Dividend-Price Ratio and Expectations of Future Dividends and Discount Factors (Campbell & Shiller, 1988)

The Ranked Asset Allocation Model: When Passive Meets Active

Posted on February 8, 2026

The Question That Started It All

Imagine you have built the perfect diversified portfolio. Twelve carefully selected funds spanning US stocks, international equities, real estate, commodities, bonds from around the world, and cash. Each fund gets an equal slice of your money, creating a beautifully balanced allocation. You set it and forget it, rebalancing once a year to maintain those equal weights. This is passive investing at its finest.

Now imagine the market crashes. Your portfolio stays fully invested, riding the entire decline down. Or imagine a roaring bull market where certain assets are clearly outperforming while others lag. Your portfolio maintains the same allocation to winners and losers alike. Does this seem optimal? Should a well designed portfolio simply ignore changing market conditions?

This is the question that Italian financial analyst Gioele Giordano grappled with when developing what would become the 2018 Charles H. Dow Award winning Ranked Asset Allocation Model. What if you could keep the solid foundation of a diversified passive portfolio but make it responsive to market conditions? What if the portfolio could automatically shift toward strength and away from weakness while maintaining systematic discipline?

Understanding the Building Blocks

Before we dive into the sophisticated ranking system, we need to understand what a portfolio actually is and why diversification matters. A portfolio is simply your collection of investments. Think of it as a basket holding different types of assets like stocks, bonds, real estate, and commodities. The fundamental principle is diversification, which means not putting all your eggs in one basket. When stocks fall, bonds might rise. When domestic markets struggle, international markets might thrive. When inflation hurts bonds, real estate and commodities might provide protection.

The eternal debate in investing is whether to manage your portfolio actively or passively. Passive management means buying a diversified mix and holding it no matter what happens in the market. This approach assumes markets are efficient and that trying to beat them is futile. Active management means constantly adjusting based on market conditions, believing that careful analysis can identify opportunities to outperform.

The data tells an interesting story. In just the first half of 2017, nearly five hundred billion dollars flowed out of actively managed funds into passive funds. People were fed up with high fees and managers who failed to beat their benchmarks. But this massive shift happened during an unusual period of very low market volatility, partially due to central bank intervention. What looked like a permanent new normal might actually be temporary. When volatility returns, purely passive strategies that never adjust could face serious challenges.

The 7Twelve Portfolio Foundation

Enter the 7Twelve Portfolio, created by Craig Israelsen in 2008. Instead of the traditional sixty forty stock bond split, this portfolio spreads risk across twelve different funds representing seven asset classes. The genius is in its comprehensive diversification.

The portfolio includes three US equity funds covering large cap, mid cap, and small cap stocks. It holds two international equity funds for developed and emerging markets. There is a real estate fund, two commodity and natural resource funds, two US bond funds including inflation protected securities, an international bond fund, and a short term treasury fund serving as the cash position. In its core version, each fund receives exactly 8.33 percent of the portfolio.

This equal weighting ensures no single asset class dominates. The diversification is remarkable. When one area struggles, others often thrive. Historical testing shows the 7Twelve Portfolio delivered smoother returns than concentrated portfolios while maintaining competitive performance. But here is the catch: it is purely passive. The allocation never changes regardless of market conditions. During the 2008 financial crisis, it stayed fully invested through the entire crash. During subsequent bull markets, it held the same allocation even as certain assets significantly outperformed others.

The Innovation: Making It Dynamic

The Ranked Asset Allocation Model uses the exact same twelve funds as the 7Twelve Portfolio. The difference is that instead of maintaining fixed allocations, it employs a sophisticated ranking system to determine which assets deserve higher allocations each month. The model evaluates each asset on four critical dimensions: momentum, volatility, correlation, and trend strength.

Momentum: Riding the Trends

Momentum measures how much an asset's price has changed over recent months. The model calculates four month momentum using daily returns. Why does this matter? Decades of financial research show that trends persist. Stocks that have been rising often continue rising for several more months. Those falling tend to keep falling. This contradicts the idea that markets instantly reflect all information. Instead, prices adjust gradually as investors slowly recognize and react to changing conditions.

By favoring assets with strong positive momentum, the model positions itself to ride favorable trends. Assets showing the strongest momentum receive higher rankings and potentially larger allocations.

Volatility: Measuring Stability

Not all returns are created equal. An asset returning ten percent with wild daily swings is fundamentally different from one returning ten percent smoothly. Volatility measures these fluctuations. The model uses a sophisticated approach based on GARCH, which stands for Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity. Do not let the name intimidate you. The key insight is simple: volatility is not constant. It changes over time in somewhat predictable patterns.

When volatility is low, it tends to stay low for a while. When it spikes, it often remains elevated. The model uses this insight through a ten day smoothed volatility measure. Assets with lower volatility receive higher rankings because they represent more stable, predictable returns. During uncertain market periods, these steady performers become particularly valuable.

Correlation: True Diversification

Correlation measures how different assets move relative to each other. If two assets always move together, they do not provide real diversification. The model calculates the average correlation of each asset with all other assets over a four month period. Assets with low average correlation receive higher rankings because they provide genuine diversification benefits.

This becomes critical during market stress when correlations often increase and everything tends to fall together. By continuously favoring low correlation assets, the model maintains diversification when it matters most. You want assets that zig when others zag, especially during turbulent times.

Trend and Breakout System: The Safety Net

The fourth component uses the Average True Range to create dynamic bands around each asset's price. When price breaks above the upper band, it signals unusual strength. When it breaks below the lower band, it warns of potential trouble. This provides an early warning system, helping the model identify when assets are entering strong uptrends or dangerous downtrends.

Unlike the other factors that contribute to the ranking, this component acts more like a filter, adjusting the final score to reflect whether an asset is in a healthy trend or showing signs of breakdown.

How the Monthly Ranking Works

Every month, the model evaluates all eleven funds, excluding the cash position. Each fund receives a rank from one to eleven based on each of the three main factors. The fund with the highest momentum gets rank eleven for momentum, while the lowest gets rank one. For volatility, it flips: the fund with the lowest volatility gets rank eleven because lower volatility is better. Same with correlation: lower average correlation earns a higher rank.

These individual rankings get combined into a total rank using this formula: total rank equals the weighted sum of the momentum rank, volatility rank, and correlation rank, minus the trend signal, plus a small momentum adjustment to prevent ties. The weights assigned to each factor can be customized based on your preferences, but the key is combining multiple dimensions rather than relying on any single measure.

After calculating total ranks, the model selects the five funds with the lowest total ranks. Why lowest? Because in this system, lower total ranks indicate the best combination of strong momentum, low volatility, and good diversification. These five become candidates for inclusion in the portfolio.

But there is one more crucial filter. Each selected fund must have positive absolute momentum, meaning its price must be higher now than four months ago. If a selected fund has negative momentum, its allocation goes to cash instead. In extreme conditions where all five selected funds show negative momentum, the entire portfolio moves to one hundred percent cash. This is your bear market protection.

The funds that pass both the ranking and momentum filters get equal weight. If all five qualify, each receives twenty percent. If only three have positive momentum, each gets roughly thirty three percent while the rest sits in cash. The allocation dynamically ranges from fully defensive in cash to fully aggressive in the top performers.

Real World Testing and Performance

Giordano tested this model from July 2004 through November 2017, comparing it against the original passive 7Twelve Portfolio, the S&P 500 index, and the Salient Risk Parity Index. The results were compelling, with the Ranked Asset Allocation Model outperforming all three benchmarks. During the 2008 financial crisis, while passive strategies stayed fully invested and suffered massive drawdowns, this model shifted to defensive positions, preserving capital. When markets recovered, it rotated back to equities, capturing the upside.

For detailed performance statistics, backtest results, and comprehensive analysis of how the model performed across different market conditions, you can review the complete research in the original paper by Gioele Giordano.

Original Paper: Ranked Asset Allocation Model (Giordano, 2018): Charles H. Dow Award Winner

Figgie: Simulating a Market in a Card Game

Posted on February 15, 2026

What if you could study the dynamics of financial markets using a card game? That is exactly the premise behind a fascinating 2021 paper by Anthony Ozerov, Steven DiSilvio, and Yu (Anna) Luo, who built an agent-based simulation around Figgie, a trading card game designed by Jane Street to mimic open-outcry commodities markets.

The result is more than a game experiment. It is a window into how different types of traders interact, how latency affects performance, and why certain strategies collapse entirely under the wrong conditions.

What is Figgie?

Figgie is played with a 40-card deck divided into four suits. One suit has 12 cards (the common suit), one has 8, and two have 10. The suit that shares a color with the 12-card suit but is not itself the common suit becomes the goal suit. Players do not know the deck composition at the start. They trade cards with each other for chips, trying to accumulate goal suit cards because at the end of the round, each goal suit card pays 10 chips from the pot, and whoever holds the majority receives a bonus of 100 or 120 chips depending on the deck.

This uncertainty is the heart of the game. Nobody knows which suit is the goal suit at the start. Prices must be discovered through trading itself, making Figgie a remarkably clean model of a real market where the true value of an asset is hidden and must be inferred.

The Simulation

The authors built a discrete-event simulation where each trader is an independent agent. Events, either an agent reconsidering whether to trade or an order being placed, occur continuously in time rather than in fixed steps. This is a meaningful design choice: real markets do not tick like a clock. Orders arrive whenever they arrive.

One clever innovation is how they handle latency. Rather than storing historical snapshots of the order book for slow agents to look up, they shift a slow agent's consideration event backward in time by its latency, so it naturally sees older market data and its order arrives delayed. Same outcome, far less computational overhead.

All agents share the same basic order-sending logic. Given an expected value for an asset, a buying agent randomly samples a price between zero and that expected value, then caps it at the current best ask to avoid overpaying. A selling agent samples a price between its expected value and twice that value, then floors it at the current best bid to avoid underselling. The strategies differ entirely in how they arrive at that expected value.

The Noise Trader

The noise trader is the simplest possible agent and serves as the baseline against which everything else is measured. It has no model of the game, no beliefs about which suit might be the goal suit, and no memory of past trades. Instead, it takes the current best bid price for an asset and multiplies it by a random log-normal factor, producing a noisy, directionless estimate of value:

\[ p^*_j = b_j e^Z, \quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) \]

where \(p^*_j\) is the noise trader's expected value for asset \(j\), \(b_j\) is the current best bid, and \(\sigma^2\) is a variance parameter controlling how noisy the estimate is. In practice, this means the noise trader is just anchoring to whatever the market currently says and adding random perturbation. It has no mechanism for learning, no way to improve its estimates over time, and no edge over any other participant.

As expected, it finishes with less wealth than fundamentalists in every simulation configuration tested. What is interesting about the noise trader, however, is its role in the ecosystem. It provides liquidity. Other strategies, particularly the bottom-feeder, can extract value precisely because noise traders are willing to trade at prices that do not reflect genuine information. In a market populated entirely by noise traders, outcomes are essentially random and the variance in final wealth is enormous, with a standard deviation of around 201 chips across 100 simulations, compared to just 62 for fundamentalists and 49 for bottom-feeders.

The Fundamentalist

The fundamentalist is the most sophisticated strategy in the paper and consistently the best performer. It builds a genuine probabilistic model of the game state and updates that model continuously as new information arrives through observed trades.

Recall that there are 12 possible deck configurations in Figgie, each specifying a different suit as the goal suit. At the start of the game, the fundamentalist treats all 12 as equally likely. But as trades occur, it tracks every card it has seen change hands, keeping a running tally of how many distinct cards of each suit have appeared across all players. Using combinatorics, it calculates how likely each of the 12 deck configurations is to have produced the observed distribution of cards, constructing a full posterior probability distribution over possible decks. Call this distribution \(m\), where \(m_i\) is the probability that deck \(i\) is currently in play.

From this posterior, the fundamentalist derives two separate expected values for each suit, one for buying and one for selling:

\[ e_b(j, j_n, m) = \sum_{i=0}^{11} m_i \cdot v(i, j, j_n) \]
\[ e_s(j, j_n, m) = e_b(j, j_n - 1, m) \]

where \(j\) is the suit, \(j_n\) is how many cards of that suit the agent currently holds, and \(v(i, j, j_n)\) measures the value of buying one more card of suit \(j\) assuming deck \(i\) is in play and you already have \(j_n\) cards. The sell value is simply the buy value shifted down by one card, which captures the intuition that selling a card when you hold \(j_n\) costs exactly as much as buying one when you hold \(j_n - 1\).

This asymmetry is essential and reflects something genuinely true about the game. The value of acquiring one more card depends critically on how many you already hold. If you have four cards of the goal suit and need six to guarantee a majority, buying a fifth card brings you meaningfully closer to that bonus. But if you already hold seven and the majority threshold is six, buying an eighth gives you almost no additional value. Conversely, selling your sixth card when you hold exactly six is catastrophic because it costs you the majority bonus entirely.

The paper captures this with a geometric weighting scheme for the majority component of each card's value:

\[ v_m(i, j_n) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{p_i(1-r)}{1-r^{x_i}} \cdot r^{j_n} & \text{if } j_n < x_i \\ 0 & \text{if } j_n \geq x_i \end{cases} \]

where \(x_i\) is the number of goal suit cards needed to guarantee a majority under deck \(i\), \(p_i\) is the majority bonus payout, and \(r\) is an agent-specific parameter greater than 1. The key insight is that the contribution of each additional card toward securing a majority scales geometrically with how many you already hold. The closer you are to the threshold, the more each additional card is worth. Once you have already secured a majority, the term drops to zero entirely.

One important operational detail is that the fundamentalist continuously cancels its own stale orders through lazy deletion. Because its expected values can shift dramatically as new trades reveal information about the deck, orders placed even a short time ago may no longer reflect the agent's current beliefs. Leaving them in the book would expose the fundamentalist to being picked off at outdated prices. This kind of active order management mirrors real market making behavior.

The result is an agent that genuinely understands what it is trading, adapts its valuations as evidence accumulates, and prices the asymmetry between buying and selling correctly. In every combination of strategies tested, the fundamentalist finishes with the highest average wealth.

The Bottom-Feeder

The bottom-feeder is the social trader of the group, and its design is both elegant and deeply parasitic. Rather than reasoning about the underlying game, it watches what other agents are doing and infers value from their behavior. Specifically, it selects one or more agents as its prey, observes their most recent buy and sell orders for each suit, and averages those prices to construct its own expected value estimate:

\[ p^*_j = \frac{1}{|A|} \sum_{i \in A} \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{1}{k} \sum_{p_b \in B_{ji}(k)} p_b + \frac{1}{k} \sum_{p_s \in S_{ji}(k)} p_s \right) \]

where \(A\) is the set of prey agents, \(k\) is how many recent orders to examine per agent (set to 4 in the paper), and \(B_{ji}(k)\) and \(S_{ji}(k)\) are the \(k\) most recent buy and sell orders of prey agent \(i\) for suit \(j\). In plain English: take the average of each prey agent's recent buy and sell prices, then average those averages across all prey.

The intuition is straightforward: if the fundamentalist has been consistently buying hearts at prices around 15 chips, it probably believes hearts are likely the goal suit and has priced that belief into its orders. The bottom-feeder does not need to do any of the Bayesian card counting itself. It simply reads the fundamentalist's revealed preferences and mirrors them, arriving at similar conclusions one step later and at much lower computational cost.

When its prey is a fundamentalist, the bottom-feeder performs meaningfully better than noise traders but falls short of the fundamentalist itself. The gap exists because the bottom-feeder is always slightly behind. By the time it observes the fundamentalist's orders and updates its own estimate, the market has already partially moved. It is extracting value from the fundamentalist's information processing without bearing the cost of that processing, but it can never outperform its source.

When the bottom-feeder preys on noise traders, results are more mixed. Noise trader orders carry little genuine information, so averaging them produces estimates that are not much better than noise. The bottom-feeder in this configuration roughly matches the noise trader in final cash but does slightly better on the end-of-game bonus, suggesting it is at least somewhat better at accumulating goal suit cards.

The most striking finding about the bottom-feeder comes from homogeneous setups where all four players are bottom-feeders. In this configuration, no trades occur at all. Every bottom-feeder waits to observe someone else's orders before forming an estimate, but no one ever goes first. The market freezes. This is a perfect illustration of a coordination failure: a strategy that works by free-riding on others collapses entirely when everyone tries to free-ride simultaneously.

Why the Chartist Fails

The most instructive failure in the paper belongs to the chartist, the trader who looks only at recent price trends and extrapolates forward. Two implementations were tested, one based on average log returns over a rolling window and one using linear regression on recent prices, and both broke down completely. The estimated values grew so large they could not be stored as double-precision floating point numbers.

The reason is a feedback loop unique to small markets. In Figgie there are only four traders and trades happen in the dozens or hundreds, not millions. When a chartist slightly overestimates an asset's value and places a high order, that order becomes the most recent price. The chartist then uses that inflated price as its new reference point, producing an even higher estimate, which produces an even higher price, and so on without bound. In large real-world markets, the sheer volume of other participants dampens this effect. In Figgie, there is no such buffer.

There is a secondary problem too. The agents in this simulation ignore risk entirely, focusing only on maximizing expected returns. In real markets, risk aversion provides a natural ceiling on how aggressively a chartist will chase a rising price. Without that ceiling, the feedback loop has nothing to stop it.

Interestingly, the autocorrelation analysis of price returns suggests that a well-designed chartist could theoretically find exploitable patterns in the Figgie market. When returns are computed without splitting into fixed time periods, the autocorrelation is generally positive and decays toward zero with increasing lag, meaning positive returns do tend to predict further positive returns in the short term. The challenge is building a strategy that exploits this without triggering the feedback loop.

The Latency Surprise

The latency results offer a counterintuitive finding. When one fundamentalist is given zero latency and three others are given a latency of 100, the fast agent actually ends up with less cash and less total wealth than its slower peers, despite receiving slightly more of the goal suit payout. The likely explanation is that lower latency means higher trading frequency, since agents begin reconsidering a new trade only after their previous order enters the book. Early in the game, when beliefs about the goal suit are still forming and expected values fluctuate wildly, trading more frequently means making more bad trades at the worst possible time. Speed is only an advantage once you know what you are doing.

Why This Matters Beyond the Game

The paper is partly an argument for agent-based simulation as a research method. Unlike backtesting on historical data, a simulation lets each strategy actually affect the market it is trading in. The fundamentalist does not just observe prices; its trades move prices, which then affect what the bottom-feeder infers. This feedback between strategies is impossible to study with historical data alone.

The bootstrap method introduced for statistical comparison is also worth noting. Because Figgie is zero-sum, the outcomes of all agents in a single game are not independent, which breaks standard statistical tests. The authors use bootstrapped confidence intervals on the difference between agents' mean outcomes, a clean solution that is applicable to any zero-sum simulation.

When Markets Become Networks: Systemic Risk and Correlation Graphs

Posted on February 22, 2026

There is a version of the 2008 financial crisis that most people know: housing prices fell, mortgage-backed securities collapsed, banks failed. It is a story told in terms of individual assets and individual institutions. But there is another version of the same story, one that I find more interesting and more unsettling, where the crisis was less about any single thing going wrong and more about how deeply everything was connected to everything else.

That second version is the one correlation network theory tries to formalize.

The Basic Idea

A correlation network takes a set of financial assets, computes pairwise correlations between their return series over some rolling window, and then draws a graph where each asset is a node and each correlation is a weighted edge. The result is not just a matrix of numbers. It is a picture of the market's hidden structure at a given point in time, a snapshot of who moves with whom and how tightly.

The early work on this goes back to Mantegna's 1999 paper in the European Physical Journal, where he applied a minimum spanning tree to extract the most economically meaningful subset of correlations from a full equity market graph. The spanning tree strips away redundant connections and leaves the skeleton, the relationships that matter most. What he found was that the resulting tree actually had economic logic: companies in the same sector clustered together naturally, without any sector labels being provided to the algorithm. The structure emerged purely from price co-movement.

That result alone should make you pause. The market, just by trading, was encoding industry structure into its correlation topology.

From Structure to Risk

The natural next question is whether you can read danger signals from that topology before they show up in prices. This is where things get genuinely interesting.

Billio, Getmansky, Lo, and Pelizzon explored this in a 2012 paper in the Journal of Financial Economics, looking at interconnectedness among hedge funds, banks, broker-dealers, and insurance companies using Granger causality networks. Their core finding was that the degree of interconnectedness across these four sectors increased substantially in the years leading up to the 2008 crisis, and that this increase was measurable well before the crisis actually materialized. The network was getting denser and more tightly coupled even as individual institutions looked reasonably healthy by traditional metrics.

This is the key insight: correlation networks can carry systemic risk information that does not show up when you look at assets in isolation. A single bond might look fine. But if that bond is now highly correlated with fifty other bonds that are each highly correlated with each other, the system as a whole has become fragile in a way that no single balance sheet reveals.

What Changes Over Time

One of the most useful properties of correlation networks is that they are dynamic. The same set of assets will have very different network structure in a calm market versus a stressed one. In normal times, correlations between equities in different sectors tend to be moderate and varied, producing a relatively sparse, distributed network. In a crisis, correlations spike across the board as everything falls together, and the network collapses into something much more densely connected and centralized.

Researchers have tried to capture this regime shift using various topological summary statistics: the average shortest path length between nodes, the clustering coefficient, the degree distribution of the graph, entropy measures on the correlation matrix itself. Each of these tells you something slightly different about the network's character. A rising clustering coefficient, for instance, means assets are increasingly co-moving in tight local groups, even if the average pairwise correlation has not yet exploded. That can be an earlier signal than correlation alone.

There is also work on what happens to the eigenvalue spectrum of the correlation matrix as markets transition between regimes. Under the random matrix theory benchmark, most eigenvalues of a correlation matrix built from uncorrelated assets fall within a predictable band. When eigenvalues start falling outside that band, particularly when the largest eigenvalue grows relative to the rest, it means genuine common structure has emerged in the data. A market-wide shock factor is pulling everything together. Tracking this over time gives you a continuous measure of how much the market is behaving like a single entity versus a collection of loosely related parts.

The Imputation Problem

One practical challenge that does not get enough attention in the introductory literature is that real rolling correlation matrices are often incomplete or numerically unreliable. Yield series go missing for certain maturities on certain dates. Short windows produce noisy estimates. And some methods of handling these issues, like filling in with zeros or using simple averaging, can quietly break the positive definiteness of the matrix, which has downstream consequences for any model that consumes it.

Preserving positive definiteness is not just a mathematical nicety. A valid correlation matrix is one that actually could have been produced by some joint distribution of the underlying variables. An invalid one is an artifact, and training a model on artifacts teaches it the wrong things. This is why matrix completion techniques that explicitly enforce positive-definiteness constraints matter more than they might initially seem.

Why This Framework Appeals to Me

What I find compelling about network-based approaches is that they take seriously the relational structure of financial data, rather than treating assets as isolated scalar series. A stock is not just its own return history. It exists in a web of co-movement relationships with everything else in the market, and those relationships are themselves informative and dynamic.

There is a deeper point here too. Classical financial models often assume that correlations are either fixed or follow simple parametric processes. The empirical record is unkind to that assumption. Correlations change, and they change in ways that are themselves patterned. Learning to read those patterns, rather than averaging them away, seems like one of the more interesting open problems at the intersection of mathematics and finance.

I have been spending a lot of time with these ideas lately, and they keep showing up as a useful lens for thinking about other problems too. More on that soon.

Level, Slope, Curvature: What the Yield Curve Is Actually Saying

Posted on March 1, 2026

If you follow financial news at all, you have heard about yield curve inversion. The two-year Treasury yield rises above the ten-year yield, commentators start using the word "recession," and then everyone waits to see whether the signal is real this time. It is a useful shorthand. But it compresses an enormous amount of information into a single number, and I think that compression loses something important.

The yield curve is not one thing. It is a surface, a continuous function mapping maturities to yields at every point in time. Watching just one spread is like trying to understand ocean weather by measuring the height of a single wave.

The Nelson-Siegel Framework

The most elegant solution to this dimensionality problem that I have encountered is the Nelson-Siegel model, introduced in a 1987 paper in the Journal of Business. The core idea is disarmingly simple: fit the entire yield curve at each point in time using just three factors, which they call level, slope, and curvature.

The mathematical form represents the yield at maturity τ as a weighted combination of three loading functions:

\[ y_t(\tau) = \beta_{0t} + \beta_{1t} \left(\frac{1 - e^{-\lambda\tau}}{\lambda\tau}\right) + \beta_{2t} \left(\frac{1 - e^{-\lambda\tau}}{\lambda\tau} - e^{-\lambda\tau}\right) \]

The first factor, β₀, loads equally on all maturities. It shifts the entire curve up or down without changing its shape. This is the level. The second factor, β₁, has a loading that starts at one for very short maturities and decays toward zero for long ones. It tilts the curve, steepening or flattening it. This is the slope. The third factor, β₂, has a loading that starts at zero, rises to a hump in the middle maturities, and then falls back toward zero at the long end. It controls the curve's belly. This is the curvature.

Three numbers. That is the whole yield curve, to a remarkably good approximation.

What Each Factor Means Economically

The reason this decomposition is useful rather than just mathematically convenient is that each factor has a genuine economic interpretation.

The level factor tracks long-run inflation expectations and the long-run neutral real rate. When the market believes that inflation will be persistently higher over the next decade, long-term yields rise and the level factor increases. It is a slow-moving variable, reflecting beliefs about the macroeconomic regime rather than short-term fluctuations.

The slope factor is what drives the recession signal that gets all the press attention. It reflects the stance of monetary policy relative to growth expectations. When the central bank tightens aggressively, short rates rise faster than long rates, and the slope factor becomes negative, meaning the curve inverts. That inversion signals that markets believe current tight policy will eventually lead to slower growth and subsequent easing. The 2-10 spread you read about is essentially a noisy proxy for this factor.

The curvature factor is the least-discussed of the three, which is a shame because it often carries early signals that the other two miss. It captures medium-term rate uncertainty, the question of what the economy will look like in the two-to-five year window. A pronounced hump in the yield curve, meaning mid-maturity yields sitting above both short and long yields, often reflects elevated uncertainty about the path of policy normalization. It does not scream recession the way an inversion does, but it whispers.

The Arbitrage-Free Extension

The original Nelson-Siegel model is purely a curve-fitting exercise. It does not care about no-arbitrage constraints, the requirement that you cannot construct a portfolio of bonds that generates risk-free profit. For descriptive purposes, that is fine. But if you want to use the model to price derivatives, generate risk-neutral forecasts, or feed factor estimates into a larger financial model, you need to be more careful.

Christensen, Diebold, and Rudebusch addressed this in a 2011 paper in the Journal of Econometrics, showing how to impose no-arbitrage structure on the Nelson-Siegel framework without sacrificing its empirical tractability. The key modification is in how the dynamics of the factors are modeled under the risk-neutral measure. The result is what is now called the Arbitrage-Free Nelson-Siegel model, or AFNS, which retains the three-factor interpretation while being consistent with the absence of free money.

In practice, this matters because the estimated factor paths under AFNS are smoother and more economically coherent than those from unconstrained curve fitting. The no-arbitrage discipline acts as a regularizer on the factor dynamics, preventing the model from fitting noise in individual maturities by making implausible moves in the underlying factors.

Why Three Factors Are Not Always Enough

It is worth being honest about where the framework breaks down. The Nelson-Siegel decomposition is a linear one. It assumes that the yield curve's shape at any given time can be represented as a linear combination of the three fixed loading functions. In most market conditions, this is a reasonable approximation. But during periods of severe stress, or when central bank policy is operating in unconventional ways, the residuals from a three-factor fit can be substantial and patterned in ways that matter.

There is also the question of what information is lost by collapsing the yield curve into three numbers before feeding it into a forecasting model. If you are interested in which specific maturity segments are tightening or decoupling from each other, a three-factor summary may not capture that detail. This is precisely why approaches that preserve the full cross-maturity correlation structure, rather than projecting it onto a low-dimensional basis, can pick up signals that Nelson-Siegel misses.

The most interesting models, in my view, are ones that use both simultaneously: the Nelson-Siegel factors provide macro-consistent anchors that prevent the model from drifting into economically nonsensical territory, while richer representations of the correlation structure between maturities carry the granular signal. Neither alone is as powerful as the combination.

A Final Thought

One of the things I keep returning to is how much the choice of representation matters in quantitative work. The yield curve inverts or it does not: that is one bit of information. The level is at 4.2%, the slope is minus 0.3%, the curvature is 0.8%: that is a richer picture, but still a compression. The full correlation matrix of twelve spreads evolving over time: richer still, but harder to interpret without guidance.

The honest answer is that we probably need all of these representations talking to each other, and figuring out how to make that conversation precise is one of the more interesting open problems I know of.

The Affine Arbitrage-Free Class of Nelson–Siegel Term Structure Models (Christensen, Diebold & Rudebusch, 2007 NBER Working Paper)

The Kelly Criterion: How to Size Your Bets to Grow Wealth Fastest

Posted on March 8, 2026

The Problem With Betting

Imagine you are offered a wager on a coin flip. The coin is slightly biased in your favor. If it comes up heads, you win two dollars. If tails, you lose one dollar. The probability of heads is 60 percent and tails is 40 percent. This is a mathematically favorable bet. Your expected value is 0.6(2) + 0.4(−1) = 0.8 dollars, which means on average you make money.

Now the question becomes urgent in a way that traditional finance theory does not quite prepare you for: How much of your wealth should you wager on each flip?

The intuitive answer is often all in, or at least bet as much as possible to maximize the expected value. But if you did that and the coin came up tails early, you would lose everything and be done. The risk is ruin. Your expected value over many repeated bets vanishes if you do not get to repeat the bets at all because you ran out of capital after a bad streak.

On the other end, if you bet nothing, you make no money, which is also suboptimal. Somewhere between recklessness and excessive caution lies an optimal fraction of your wealth to wager on each bet. In 1956, John Kelly Jr., a researcher at Bell Laboratories, published a paper that derived exactly what that fraction should be. The answer has profound implications for investing, gambling, and any repeated decision under uncertainty.

The Kelly Formula

Suppose you have a series of wagering opportunities, each with a fixed probability p of winning a fixed payoff and probability q = 1 − p of losing your stake. The odds are such that if you win, you gain b dollars for every one dollar wagered. If you lose, you lose one dollar.

The Kelly criterion gives the optimal fraction f* of your bankroll to wager on each bet:

\[ f^* = \frac{bp - q}{b} = \frac{bp - (1-p)}{b} = p - \frac{1-p}{b} \]

Or equivalently, written as:

\[ f^* = \frac{p}{b} - \frac{1-p}{b} \]

Let us verify this makes sense with our coin flip example. Here p = 0.6, the payoff means you win 2 dollars for every 1 wagered, so b = 2, and q = 0.4. Plugging in:

\[ f^* = \frac{0.6 \times 2 - 0.4}{2} = \frac{1.2 - 0.4}{2} = \frac{0.8}{2} = 0.4 \]

The Kelly formula says you should wager 40 percent of your bankroll on each flip. Not all in. Not 1 percent. But 40 percent.

Why This Fraction is Optimal

The way to think about Kelly is through the lens of logarithmic utility of wealth. Kelly asked a different question than standard expected value maximization: What betting strategy maximizes the exponential growth rate of your wealth over time?

If you wager a fraction f of your wealth on a bet with probability p of winning b dollars per dollar wagered, your wealth after one bet is:

\[ W = W_0 \begin{cases} (1 + bf) & \text{with probability } p \\ (1 - f) & \text{with probability } q \end{cases} \]

Over many independent bets, the long run growth rate is determined by the geometric mean of the multipliers:

\[ G(f) = p \ln(1 + bf) + q \ln(1 - f) \]

The Kelly criterion maximizes this growth function. Taking the derivative with respect to f and setting it to zero yields the optimal fraction f*.

Why growth rate and not just expected value? Because when you are playing repeated games, the order of wins and losses matters. A catastrophic loss of 50 percent in the early game, followed by later gains, puts you in a worse position than the same expected value but in a different sequence. Compounding works in your favor when you avoid large losses that never recover. The Kelly criterion minimizes the risk of catastrophic drawdowns while still growing wealth exponentially over repeated plays. It is the sweet spot.

Portfolio Management and Fractional Kelly

The Kelly criterion has attracted intense interest in finance, particularly in portfolio management and quantitative investing. In 1969, Edward Thorp and Sheen Kassouf applied Kelly ideas to the stock market and options. Their mathematical framework showed that an analogous principle applies even with multiple correlated assets and time-varying returns.

In modern portfolio theory, Kelly betting is sometimes called "full Kelly." Many practitioners use a fraction of the Kelly recommendation, called "fractional Kelly," typically betting half to a quarter of the Kelly amount. Why the caution?

The answer is that Kelly has teeth. It is optimal only if your probability estimates are correct. But in real markets, you do not know p and b with certainty. You estimate them. Small errors in estimating p compound into large errors in sizing. If you believe you have an edge that is really smaller than you think, full Kelly can lead to spectacular losses. Using fractional Kelly acts as a regulator. It sacrifices some optimal growth in exchange for protection against estimation error. Half Kelly, for instance, still grows your wealth faster than most conventional approaches while cutting the peak drawdown nearly in half.

The Pitfalls and Criticisms

Kelly is powerful but not magical, and it carries important caveats. The first is the assumption problem I mentioned. Kelly is only optimal if you have the true probability distribution. In investing, you almost never do. You estimate a win rate and payoff ratio from historical data. Historical estimates vary. A small error in p throws off the whole calculation. This is why fractional Kelly is pragmatic wisdom rather than departure from theory.

The second is the horizon problem. Kelly is a long-run criterion. It is designed for repeated bets played to infinity. If your investing horizon is finite, or if you have a hard stop loss constraint you must respect, the pure Kelly formula may not be appropriate. A portfolio sizedown event that would be merely temporary in the infinite horizon case might force you out of the market permanently in the finite case.

The third is the model specification problem. Kelly assumes fixed odds and fixed probability. Most real markets have neither. Volatility regimes shift. Win rates change. Correlations between bets vary over time. The formula does not account for these complications unless you embed them into a more sophisticated model.

Nevertheless, Kelly's core insight stands: there is an optimal fraction of capital to commit, and it is neither all in nor nothing. That insight alone is worth its weight in research papers.

Kelly in Practice

In quantitative trading, Kelly sizing is often applied to uncorrelated bets or small-cap strategies where you have relative confidence in your edge. Large hedge funds sometimes use fractional Kelly as a core discipline. Insurance companies, which write many independent risks, implicitly use Kelly-like principles in their capital allocation.

For individual investors, the main takeaway is not the formula itself, which is hard to apply without precise probability estimates, but the principle: bet sizing matters. Avoid all in. Avoid timidity. Find the middle ground where you can survive a bad streak and still compound your wealth. Kelly is the mathematical expression of what that middle ground looks like.

Original Paper: A New Interpretation of Information Rate (Kelly, J. L., 1956)

Indicators That Matter: From Fibonacci to ICT

Posted on April 26, 2026

Technical analysis has a credibility problem in academic circles. Most of it is dismissed as pattern recognition dressed up in jargon. Some of that dismissal is earned. But a subset of technical tools have survived long enough, been refined enough, and are used by enough serious market participants that ignoring them entirely seems like its own kind of intellectual laziness. This post is an attempt to take seven of those tools seriously, explain the mechanics precisely, and connect them where possible to what the empirical literature actually says.

1. Fibonacci Retracement

The Fibonacci sequence is constructed by the recurrence \(F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}\) with \(F_0 = 0,\ F_1 = 1\). As \(n \to \infty\), the ratio \(F_n / F_{n-1}\) converges to the golden ratio \(\phi \approx 1.618\). The retracement levels used in trading — 23.6%, 38.2%, 50%, 61.8%, and 78.6% — are all derived from ratios within the sequence:

\[ \phi = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} \approx 1.618, \qquad \frac{1}{\phi} \approx 0.618, \qquad 1 - \frac{1}{\phi} \approx 0.382 \]

Given a price move from a swing low \(P_L\) to a swing high \(P_H\), the retracement level at ratio \(r\) is:

\[ P_r = P_H - r \cdot (P_H - P_L) \]

The 61.8% level is the most closely watched because it equals \(1/\phi\) and represents a "deep" retracement that still preserves the structure of the prior move. The 38.2% and 50% levels act as shallower pullback zones. Whether these levels have genuine predictive power or are self-fulfilling prophecies due to widespread use is an open question — Bhattacharya and Bhattacharya (2013) found statistically significant price reactions at 61.8% retracements in equity indices, but the effect sizes are modest and sensitive to the lookback window used to identify swings.

2. Smart Money Concepts (SMC)

Smart Money Concepts is a framework, not a single indicator. The central premise is that institutional participants — banks, funds, central bank desks — leave detectable footprints in price action, and that retail traders consistently lose because they trade against institutional order flow without realizing it.

The core structural vocabulary is: Break of Structure (BOS), which occurs when price closes beyond a prior swing high or low and confirms a directional shift; and Change of Character (CHoCH), which is a BOS against the prevailing trend and signals a potential reversal. These are not arbitrary — they map roughly onto the classical Dow Theory definition of trend, where a trend is only broken when it makes a lower high and lower low (downtrend) or higher low and higher high (uptrend).

SMC is largely a practitioner framework without a canonical academic paper behind it. Its intellectual ancestor is Wyckoff's accumulation/distribution theory from the 1930s, which has received some empirical support in studies of institutional order flow. The honest assessment is that SMC provides a structured vocabulary for reading price action, but its edge is difficult to verify without access to proprietary order flow data.

3. Liquidity Sweep

A liquidity sweep, sometimes called a stop hunt, refers to a sharp price move that briefly breaches a prior swing high or low — triggering the stop-loss orders clustered just beyond it — before reversing. The mechanism relies on the predictability of where retail traders place stops: just outside obvious levels.

If \(P_{swing}\) is a prior swing high, stop orders cluster in the zone \([P_{swing}, P_{swing} + \epsilon]\). A sweep drives price into that zone, executes those stops (converting them into market sell orders that add to the downward pressure), then reverses once institutional buying absorbs the liquidity. The result is a wick on the candlestick that penetrates the level but a close back below it.

Empirically, the existence of stop clustering near technical levels is well-documented. Osler (2003) showed using foreign exchange dealer data that stop-loss orders are significantly concentrated near round numbers and prior swing points, and that price tends to accelerate through these levels when they are breached, consistent with a feedback loop between stops and momentum.

\[ \text{Sweep Signal: } P_{low} < P_{swing\_low} \text{ and } \text{Close} > P_{swing\_low} \]

4. Volume Profile

Volume Profile is a distribution of traded volume across price levels rather than across time. For a given period, you compute the total volume transacted at each price \(p\):

\[ V(p) = \sum_{t} \text{Vol}_t \cdot \mathbf{1}[P_t = p] \]

The resulting histogram reveals the Point of Control (POC), the price level with the highest traded volume; the Value Area (VA), the price range containing a specified percentage (typically 70%) of total volume; and Low Volume Nodes (LVNs), price levels where little volume traded and which price tends to move through quickly.

The theoretical foundation is market profile theory, developed by J. Peter Steidlmayer at the Chicago Board of Trade in the 1980s. The key insight is that markets spend time at prices where they facilitate trade and reject prices where they do not. High-volume nodes represent accepted value; low-volume nodes represent price levels the market transited rapidly. The POC and value area boundaries act as gravitational centers that price tends to rotate back toward.

Empirical support comes from studies of market microstructure showing that volume imbalances at price levels predict short-term price reversion. Karpoff (1987) established the positive correlation between volume and price change magnitude, which underlies the idea that volume-heavy nodes carry more structural significance than price action alone suggests.

5. Fair Value Gap (FVG)

A Fair Value Gap is a three-candle pattern where the middle candle moves so aggressively that it leaves an imbalance between the high of the first candle and the low of the third candle. Formally, for candles \(i-1\), \(i\), and \(i+1\):

\[ \text{Bullish FVG: } \text{Low}_{i+1} > \text{High}_{i-1} \]
\[ \text{Bearish FVG: } \text{High}_{i+1} < \text{Low}_{i-1} \]

The gap \([\text{High}_{i-1},\ \text{Low}_{i+1}]\) represents a price range where only one side of the market traded — the aggressive candle \(i\) moved through it so quickly that no two-sided price discovery occurred. The hypothesis is that price will return to "fill" this gap to complete the auction process.

This is structurally related to the concept of order imbalance in microstructure literature. When a large aggressive order moves price through a range without contra-side participation, market makers who were unable to participate at those levels may re-post orders there, creating a gravitational pull back toward the gap. The academic closest to this is the literature on gaps and gap-fills in equities, where studies consistently find mean-reversion tendencies within gap zones, particularly for intraday gaps created on high volume.

6. ICT (Inner Circle Trader) Methodology

ICT, developed by Michael Huddleston, is a comprehensive market framework that synthesizes many of the above concepts — liquidity sweeps, FVGs, and institutional order flow — into a time-based structure. Its distinguishing feature is the emphasis on specific time windows called Kill Zones: the London Open (2–5 AM EST), New York Open (7–10 AM EST), and London Close (10 AM–12 PM EST). The claim is that institutional order flow is concentrated in these windows, producing higher-probability setups.

The ICT framework also introduces the concept of Optimal Trade Entry (OTE), which combines a Fibonacci retracement (specifically the 62–79% zone) with a prior liquidity sweep and an FVG, creating a confluence entry zone:

\[ \text{OTE Zone} = \left[ P_H - 0.79(P_H - P_L),\ P_H - 0.62(P_H - P_L) \right] \]

The Kill Zone timing hypothesis has a partial empirical basis. Andersen and Bollerslev (1998) documented that FX volatility follows intraday patterns closely tied to market open times, with the highest activity clustering around the London and New York opens. Whether this translates into consistent directional edge is harder to verify from public data.

7. EMA, Supply & Demand

The Exponential Moving Average weights recent prices more heavily than older ones. For a smoothing factor \(\alpha = 2/(n+1)\):

\[ \text{EMA}_t = \alpha \cdot P_t + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \text{EMA}_{t-1} \]

Common configurations are the 9/21 EMA cross for short-term momentum, the 50 EMA as a dynamic support/resistance level, and the 200 EMA as the institutional benchmark for trend. The EMA's advantage over a simple moving average is its responsiveness: it assigns exponentially decaying weights \(\alpha(1-\alpha)^k\) to price \(k\) periods ago, so recent dislocations register faster.

Supply and demand zones are price ranges where price made a sharp departure — either an aggressive rally (demand zone) or an aggressive drop (supply zone) — implying that a significant order was placed in that region. The hypothesis is that if the order was only partially filled, unfilled portions remain resting at those levels, and price will react when it returns:

\[ \text{Demand Zone} = [\text{Base Low},\ \text{Base High}] \text{ preceding a strong bullish move} \]

Supply and demand zones are closely related to volume profile nodes and FVGs. All three are different lenses on the same underlying phenomenon: price levels where order flow was imbalanced and where unfinished business may remain. The distinction is methodological — volume profile uses actual volume data, FVGs use candlestick geometry, and supply/demand zones use the magnitude of the subsequent departure as a proxy for order significance.

Empirical support for EMA-based strategies is mixed in academic literature but consistently positive in practitioner research. Faber (2007) showed that a simple 10-month moving average applied to asset allocation improved risk-adjusted returns significantly over buy-and-hold, primarily by reducing drawdowns rather than increasing returns. The mechanism is trend-following persistence, not prediction.

A Unifying Observation

What is striking about these seven tools is that they are, at root, different approaches to the same underlying question: where are the significant order clusters in the market, and what happens when price reaches them? Fibonacci levels attract orders because they are watched. Volume nodes reveal where orders were historically dense. FVGs and supply/demand zones mark where orders were placed but not fully filled. Liquidity sweeps describe what happens when the market hunts those clusters. SMC and ICT provide a narrative for why institutions would engineer such moves. EMA gives a smoothed reference for the directional context in which all of this occurs.

None of these tools is a system on its own. But used together with discipline and an honest accounting of when they fail, they describe a coherent picture of how price moves through a landscape shaped by accumulated order flow.
